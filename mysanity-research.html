User Research & Personas

MySanity AI Behavioural Intelligence Platform

This section presents user insights derived from secondary research, competitor analysis, and behavioural patterns commonly observed across mental wellbeing applications. The findings informed the design of the MySanity AI experience.

Problem Statement

Users experiencing stress, anxiety, and cognitive overload lack a single, low-friction platform that provides personalised and proactive mental wellbeing support based on behavioural patterns, rather than reactive or generic advice.

Research Approach

Due to the early-stage conceptual nature of this project, user insights were developed using a combination of market research, competitor analysis, and established UX patterns within digital mental health platforms.

  • Analysis of leading mental health apps (Headspace, Calm, Wysa, Finch)
  • Review of common UX patterns in wellbeing and habit-tracking applications
  • Assumptions based on typical user behaviour in stress and anxiety management contexts
  • Evaluation of gaps in existing AI-driven mental health solutions

Target Audience

MySanity is designed for digitally engaged users experiencing stress, anxiety, or cognitive overload in high-demand environments across key global regions.

  • Age Range: 18–40
  • Regions: United Kingdom, United States, Canada, Australia, Western Europe
  • User Types: Students, early-career professionals, healthcare workers, and high-pressure roles
  • Behaviour: Mobile-first users familiar with productivity and wellbeing apps

Key Insights

  • Users struggle with generic wellbeing advice lacking personal relevance.
  • High cognitive load reduces long-term engagement in mental health apps.
  • Users prefer simple, consistent tracking over complex input flows.
  • Strong demand exists for AI-driven personalisation based on behavioural data.
  • Emotional safety and non-judgemental tone are critical for trust.
  • Fragmentation across apps disrupts consistent wellbeing routines.
  • There is a growing need for proactive mental health support, not just reactive tools.

User Needs

  • Low-effort interaction with minimal cognitive load
  • Personalised guidance based on behavioural patterns
  • Emotional safety and supportive AI tone
  • Consistency across wellbeing routines in one platform
  • Proactive support before stress escalates

Opportunity Areas

  • AI-driven early detection of stress patterns
  • Unified wellbeing system replacing fragmented tools
  • Passive behavioural tracking to reduce user effort
  • Voice-first interaction for high-stress or time-constrained moments
  • Adaptive interventions based on real-time emotional state

User Personas

Persona 1 — Sarah (Student)

  • Age: 22
  • Context: University student managing anxiety and academic pressure
  • Goals: Improve mental wellbeing, sleep better, build healthy routines
  • Pain Points: Overwhelmed by apps, inconsistent habits, generic advice
  • Needs: Simple tracking, personalised guidance, gentle reminders

Persona 2 — James (Professional)

  • Age: 34
  • Context: Full-time employee experiencing work-related stress
  • Goals: Manage stress, avoid burnout, improve work-life balance
  • Pain Points: Lack of time, fragmented tools, difficulty maintaining routines
  • Needs: Fast insights, voice interaction, minimal effort engagement

Persona 3 — Emily (Healthcare Worker)

  • Age: 29
  • Context: High-pressure healthcare environment with irregular shifts
  • Goals: Build resilience, maintain emotional stability, improve sleep
  • Pain Points: Burnout risk, inconsistent schedule, emotional fatigue
  • Needs: Adaptive AI support, flexible reminders, short wellbeing exercises

Design Implications

  • Prioritise low cognitive load interactions to reduce user fatigue.
  • Use behavioural data (sleep, mood, activity) for personalisation.
  • Unify fragmented wellbeing functions into a single system.
  • Ensure emotionally safe and supportive AI responses.
  • Support voice interaction for fast, low-friction engagement.
